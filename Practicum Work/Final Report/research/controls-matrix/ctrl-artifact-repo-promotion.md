# Artifact-repo staging/promotion (Artifactory / Nexus) — row 6

**Axis:** Internal promotion gate (product-bound)
**Verdicts:** Stolen credential `✓` · Trusted insider `~` · Compromised CI `~` · Direct publish `✗`

Scenario names and verdicts are fixed by [evaluation-plan.md §1, Move 1](../../../../docs/evaluation-plan.md).

## What it actually gates
An artifact-repository promotion gate gates **whether an artifact moves from a staging/dev repository to a release/prod repository — both inside the artifact-management product** (JFrog Artifactory, Sonatype Nexus). Promotion is a privileged status transition, separately permissioned from deployment: Nexus splits the *Deployer* role (deploy, close, drop a staging repo) from a distinct *Promoter* role, and Artifactory build promotion is its own action against a target repository. That is the axis: the decision governed is *may this artifact advance to the internal release repo*, on a separately-authorized promoter's action. Two consequences fall straight out of the axis. First, it is **product-bound**: the gate governs repositories *inside Artifactory/Nexus*, and the public PyPI/npm publish is a separate, later step the product does not sit in front of — so a direct registry publish that never enters the product is bypassed in full (`✗`). Second, promotion is bound to the artifact's **SHA1**, which proves the promoted bytes equal the built bytes — *identity*, not *authorization*: an authentically-built poisoned artifact carries its SHA1 through promotion unchanged. This is the **closest competitor in the matrix** — separation-of-duties over an artifact move — which is exactly why it scores `✓/~/~` rather than the straight `✗` of an origin attestation.

## Documented behavior (anchored)
> "Build Promotion allows you to change the status of a build, optionally moving or copying the build's artifacts and its dependencies to a target repository." "The Build identity knows its produced artifacts by their SHA1 value, and the build.name & build.number properties." Promotion requires **Deploy permission** for the build; without it, a "Permission Denied" error occurs.
— *Build Promotion* (docs.jfrog.com). Promotion binds artifacts by their SHA1 and changes build status against a target repo — an identity-preserving move, not an inspection of behavior.

> The staging suite provides "a temporary staging repository and to manage the promotion of components from a staging repository to a release repository," where "the certification process is done on the same binaries that will ultimately be released." The staging profile creates separate roles: "Staging: Deployer … grants the user permission to deploy components, close and drop any staging repository created by the … staging profile," while "Staging: Promoter … grants the user to right to promote staging repositories created by the … staging profile."
— *Staging* (help.sonatype.com). Deployer and Promoter are **distinct** roles — the actor who lands an artifact in staging is not, by default, the actor who promotes it to release.

> "APT29 used customized malware to inject malicious code into the SolarWinds Orion software build process that was later distributed through a normal software update." "APT29 was able to get SUNBURST signed by SolarWinds code signing certificates by injecting the malware into the SolarWinds Orion software lifecycle."
— MITRE ATT&CK, Campaign C0024 (*SolarWinds Compromise*). The malicious code was injected into the **build**, then signed and shipped — an authentically-built poisoned artifact, whose SHA1 any internal promotion would carry through unchanged.

## Per-column analysis

| Scenario | Verdict | Catches / Misses | Source |
|---|:--:|---|---|
| Stolen credential | `✓` | **Catches** the modeled ordinary-credential theft: a stolen *deployer* credential can land an artifact in staging but cannot cast the **promotion** — Nexus separates *Deployer* from *Promoter*, and Artifactory promotion is a separate privileged action requiring Deploy permission on the build. The stolen dev credential stalls in staging. ⚠ | `sonatype-nexus-staging`, `jfrog-build-promotion` |
| Trusted insider | `~` | **Catches** a lone insider *without* promote rights — an insider who can only deploy cannot unilaterally advance to release; a separately-permissioned promoter is forced. **Misses** (a) an insider who *holds* the promoter / admin role, and (b) an insider who skips the internal repo to publish straight to PyPI. ⚠ | `sonatype-nexus-staging`, `jfrog-build-promotion` |
| Compromised CI | `~` | **Catches** an auto-promoting compromised *build* stage when promotion is gated behind a separate human promoter: a poisoned artifact lands in staging and blocks on the promotion decision. **Misses** the SolarWinds case — promotion certifies build *status* and moves artifacts *by SHA1* (identity, not behavior), so an authentically-built poisoned artifact promotes unchanged; and a compromise living *in* the promotion job runs after the gate. ⚠ | `jfrog-build-promotion`, `mitre-c0024-solarwinds` |
| Direct publish | `✗` | The gate is product-bound: `twine upload` straight to PyPI never enters Artifactory/Nexus, so no promotion gate exists to fire — the structural weakness shared across the whole D column. | axis argument |

> ⚠ **Caveat (Stolen credential).** `✓` holds for what column A models — theft of an *ordinary* deployer credential (the Shai-Hulud archetype), which can push to staging but cannot cast the separately-permissioned promotion. A stolen **promoter or admin** credential is a higher, rarer precondition that promotes directly — a different attack, not a downgrade of the modeled case. A stolen *registry publish token* pushed straight to PyPI is column D's archetype, not A's.

> ⚠ **Caveat (Trusted insider).** The `~` catches the lone deploy-only insider but misses the two cases the proxy closes: an insider **holding the promoter/admin role**, and an insider who **skips the internal repo to publish directly**. Rubber-stamp promotion and a colluding quorum are **shared** with the proxy — the Deployer/Promoter split *raises the bar* (one role separation) but a real quorum (m independent, re-authenticated approvals) raises it further, and neither beats collusion (the XZ / **CORE-3** limit) — so they are not counted as this row's misses.

> ⚠ **Caveat (Compromised CI).** Promotion binds the artifact by **SHA1** — which proves the promoted bytes equal the built bytes (*identity*), not that those bytes should ship (*authorization*). In SolarWinds (the SUNBURST campaign), APT29 "inject[ed] malicious code into the SolarWinds Orion software build process" and got it "signed by SolarWinds code signing certificates"; a promoter certifying build *status* inspects neither the bytes' behavior nor a policy that they are safe, so an authentically-built poisoned artifact of this kind promotes unchanged. Separately, if the compromise lives *in* the promotion job, the gate is already satisfied and the malicious code advances.

## How the proxy beats this row
Artifact-repo promotion is the **closest competitor in the matrix** — genuine separation-of-duties (Deployer ≠ Promoter) over an artifact move, keyed on SHA1. Its two gaps are *destination* and *unit of decision*. **Destination:** promotion advances an artifact between repositories *inside Artifactory/Nexus*; it is product-bound and never sits in front of the public `twine upload`, so a direct registry publish bypasses it entirely (Direct publish `✗`). **Unit of decision:** promotion authorizes a *status transition under RBAC* — one Promoter role suffices, on a standing session, with no artifact inspection — and its SHA1 binding proves *identity, not authorization*, so a poisoned-but-authentically-built artifact promotes unchanged (Compromised CI `~`). The proxy authorizes at the **public-registry publish point**: it holds the **sole** publish credential and requires **m independent, re-authenticated approvals bound to the exact artifact by hash** at authorization time. So there is **no product-bound gate to skip** (the direct-publish escape closes), and a byte-identical poisoned artifact still fails to reach quorum because approvers evaluate *this publish*, not an internal repo move. Against the insider it raises one Promoter to *m* independent, re-authenticated approvers. Honest boundary: for **enterprise-internal distribution**, where consumers resolve from the org's own Artifactory, promotion *is* effectively the publish gate and the proxy adds little; the proxy's value is sharpest where promotion cannot follow — publishing to a third-party registry the org does not own. It does **not** claim to defeat rubber-stamping, collusion, or a payload engineered to survive review: that is the XZ case, where quorum *raises the bar but is not immunity* (Case Study B, **CORE-3**).
