# Final Presentation Video

You are required to submit a video presentation of no more than 15 minutes long. You can use other tools but you have easy access to [Kaltura Media Capture](https://mediaspace.gatech.edu/kalturacapture/launch/create) to an external site., a tool that can be used to easily record audio and/or video over slides.

The video presentation must include you as a "talking head" (e.g., in the corner of the screen). Components we'll be looking for include:

    Problem description (What/Why?)
    Solution discussion (How?)
    Evaluation (evidence that supports the efficacy of the solution)
    Real-world deployment considerations
    Limitations
    Future work

## Final Presentation Guidance

As you begin to think about your final presentation, please make sure your submission has at minimum these components:

1. the cybersecurity problem you’re trying to solve
2. your solution and the methodology used to develop it
3. evaluation of your solution
4. real-world deployment considerations
5. limitations of your solution
6. next steps/future work related to your project

Production quality is important. You are expected to be on camera (in lieu of a classroom presentation) and the final product should be well-polished. Your presentation should be engaging, easy to follow, free from audio issues, and no more than 15 minutes long.

The solution discussion carries the most weight on your grade for this assignment, and how it can be covered well will vary by project. For example, if your solution is a policy solution, walk us through what you produced and please explain it at an appropriate level. Don’t rely on just a few excerpts or snippets from your policy being pasted into a slide and consider that a thorough discussion. Conversely, scrolling through the entire document is not an effective nor professional way to present your solution either. A quality presentation of a policy's purpose and how it meets that purpose is what is needed.

If your solution involves something executable that you developed (code, hardware, etc.), walk us through the screens and functionality, show us how everything works. Demonstrations are appropriate for many projects, but a review at the code level is generally not necessary, nor is showing a command-line interface as a means of simply proving something works. It is okay to speed up (or possibly skip) portions of a demonstration where your program takes time to process something. Note, however, that this pertains only to code execution. You are not to speed up an entire presentation as a means of meeting time limitations.

The final presentation is NOT an extended progress update like the shorter videos previously submitted for your groups. For scope, it may be helpful to imagine that you are presenting your work at a conference. In such an environment, you can assume that your audience has fundamental knowledge or basic familiarity with your topic, but they are not familiar with your specific project or solution (hence the need to explain the problem you are trying to solve, etc.).

Note that AI may not be used to replace a human voice or face, except in the case of advance accommodations for disability per the syllabus. To replicate a professional public-speaking environment, please dress accordingly and avoid distracting or overly casual clothing for your recording.

For submission, the same guidance applies as found in the FAQs (external link or file attachment in Canvas). Some students find it beneficial to use an unlisted YouTube link, which then becomes a private link that you can share in the future at your discretion (take pride in your efforts). Just please be sure external links are working as you expect after submission.

Lastly, please note that there are two assignment locations to submit your final presentation, the first being the graded assignment, and the second being associated with your group discussion for review by other students. Both assignments begin with "Post Final Presentation Video..." in Canvas.
